Replit Instruction Package: Unified Clinica Identity, Adaptive Learning, Difficulty Layers, Anime RPG Draw, and Medical Education System

Goal

Improve Clinica into a stronger, more engaging, more marketable fantasy medical RPG.

Clinica should appeal to:

Nonmedical curious players
Anime/RPG players
High school health science students
Pre-nursing students
Nursing students
NCLEX preparers
Healthcare professionals
Educators

The game should not feel like a quiz app first.

It should feel like:

A fantasy healer RPG where the human body is a kingdom, disease corruption attacks each body-system region, and players summon healer heroes to restore the body through clinical reasoning.

The medical learning should be real and visible, but the emotional hook should be:

Restore the kingdom.
Summon healer heroes.
Fight disease spirits.
Read clues.
Save the patient.
Unlock regions.
Grow your team.
Learn medicine through play.

Do not rebuild the whole app.

Do not remove the Codex.

Do not remove medical terminology.

Do not make separate games for each audience.

Use one shared game with adaptive explanation layers and scalable difficulty.

---

Core Positioning

Use this main tagline:

Fight disease. Restore the body. Learn medicine through play.

Use this subline where appropriate:

Build a team of healer heroes and cleanse a kingdom shaped like the human body.

For nursing or NCLEX-facing screens, use:

Practice clinical judgment through fantasy RPG battles.

For nonmedical/curious players, use:

Learn how the body works by saving a living kingdom.

---

Main World Concept

Clinica is a kingdom shaped like the human body.

Each region represents a body system:

Air Temple = respiratory system, airway, breathing, oxygenation
River Gate = cardiovascular system, circulation, perfusion, blood pressure
Fire Ward = infection, inflammation, fever, immune response
Energy Shrine = glucose, metabolism, endocrine, nutrition
Mind Tower = neuro, psych, mental status, safety
Protection Wall = skin, safety, infection prevention, falls, barriers
Storm Observatory = electrolytes, acid-base, rhythm
Filter Basin = renal system, fluid balance, waste removal
Forge Hall = musculoskeletal system, mobility, wounds, rehab
Growth Garden = OB, pediatrics, newborns, development

Disease corruption attacks these regions.

The player leads healer heroes to restore each region.

---

Main Gameplay Explanation

The player should understand this within the first 30 seconds:

Patient Stability = how safe the patient is.
Disease Corruption = how much control the disease has.
Clues = information that helps avoid guessing.
Care Chain = Scout → Stabilize → Counter → Reassess.
Victory = keep Stability above 0 and reduce Disease Corruption to 0.
Progress = restore body regions, earn shards, summon heroes, upgrade team.

---

Learning Profiles vs Difficulty

Add two separate player settings:

Learning Profile = changes how the game explains medical concepts.
Difficulty = changes how much information is revealed and how hard battles are.

Important:

Do not mix these into one system.
A player can choose NCLEX explanations with Guided difficulty.
A player can choose Curious explanations with Clinical difficulty.

Example:

Curious + Guided = simple fantasy-first explanations with many clues visible.
NCLEX + Guided = NCLEX terminology with more clues visible for teaching.
Curious + Clinical = fantasy-first language but fewer clues and harder reasoning.
NCLEX + NCLEX = clinical judgment language with all clues hidden.

Use this pattern:

Fantasy image first.
Plain explanation second.
Medical term third.

Do not remove medical terms from Curious mode.

Do not overload Curious mode with NCLEX-style language.

Do not make NCLEX mode feel like a boring question bank.

Clinica’s originality comes from connecting fantasy concepts to real medical concepts.

Core Clue System

Each enemy should have a full clue set.

Example Air Sprite / Asthma Exacerbation has 4 clues:

1. Breathing Fast
2. O2 Glow Dim
3. Tripod Position
4. Wheezing

Difficulty determines how many clues are visible at battle start and how many must be revealed through Scout, Codex, Analyze, Reassess, or correct clinical actions.

---

Clue Visibility by Difficulty

For enemies with 4 clues:

Guided:
3 clues visible at start, 1 hidden.

Standard:
2 clues visible at start, 2 hidden.

Clinical:
1 clue visible at start, 3 hidden.

NCLEX:
0 clues visible at start, all clues hidden.

Expert:
0 clues visible at start. Some clues may evolve, appear after turns, or require specific assessments.

Example Air Sprite:

Guided:
Visible: Breathing Fast, O2 Glow Dim, Tripod Position
Hidden: Wheezing

Standard:
Visible: Breathing Fast, O2 Glow Dim
Hidden: Tripod Position, Wheezing

Clinical:
Visible: Breathing Fast
Hidden: O2 Glow Dim, Tripod Position, Wheezing

NCLEX:
Visible: none
Hidden: Breathing Fast, O2 Glow Dim, Tripod Position, Wheezing

Expert:
Visible: none

At battle start:

function getInitialVisibleClues(enemy, difficulty) {
  const visibleCount = DIFFICULTY_MODIFIERS[difficulty].visibleClues;
  return enemy.clues

    .slice(0, visibleCount);

Difficulty Modifiers

Difficulty should affect clue visibility, hint strength, enemy pressure, wrong-action penalty, and star requirements.

Do not make difficulty only about bigger numbers.

The main difficulty should come from information management and clinical reasoning.

Suggested modifiers:

const DIFFICULTY_MODIFIERS = {
  guided: {
    visibleClues: 3,
    enemyDamageMultiplier: 0.75,
    wrongActionPenalty: 0,
    hintLevel: "strong",
    careAttemptLimitFor3Star: 3,
    starTurnBonus: 1
  },
  standard: {
    visibleClues: 2,
    enemyDamageMultiplier: 1.0,
    wrongActionPenalty: 1,
    hintLevel: "normal",
    careAttemptLimitFor3Star: 2,
    starTurnBonus: 0
  },
  clinical: {
    visibleClues: 1,
    enemyDamageMultiplier: 1.15,
    wrongActionPenalty: 1.25,
    hintLevel: "light",
    careAttemptLimitFor3Star: 1,
    starTurnBonus: -1
  },
  nclex: {
    visibleClues: 0,
    enemyDamageMultiplier: 1.25,
    wrongActionPenalty: 1.5,
    hintLevel: "minimal",
    careAttemptLimitFor3Star: 0,

  },
  expert: {
    visibleClues: 0,

    hintLevel: "minimal",

  }
};

Balance carefully.

Do not make the first tutorial battle unfair.

---

Difficulty Hint Rules

Guided

Show direct hints.
Show recommended roles.
Show likely system.
Show simple objective.
Warn strongly before poor-fit actions.

Example:

This looks like an Air problem. Try revealing the hidden clue, then use an Air skill.

Standard

Show likely system and recommended roles.
Give hints after wrong actions.

Example:

The clues point toward breathing difficulty. Air actions may help.

Clinical

Show fewer hints.
Show likely system only after clue discovery or Scout.
Wrong actions have stronger penalties.

Example:

Current cues suggest a respiratory pattern. Confirm with assessment.

NCLEX

Do not reveal the likely system at first.
Use NCLEX-style clinical judgment labels.
Require player to identify cues.
Minimal hints unless the player fails or asks for help.

Example:

Recognize cues before selecting an intervention.

Expert

Add evolving clues, complications, distractors, and stricter star goals.
May include mixed-system enemies.

Example:

The patient’s condition is changing. Reassess to detect the new priority.

Clue Translation System

Every clue should teach through fantasy and medical meaning.

Example: Wheezing

Fantasy:

The windways whistle as air struggles to pass.

Simple Medical:

Wheezing can mean narrowed airways.

Nursing:

Wheezing is a respiratory cue that may indicate bronchospasm or airway narrowing.

NCLEX:

Wheezing supports impaired ventilation or bronchospasm. This should prompt airway/breathing prioritization and reassessment after intervention.

Professional:

Wheezing suggests airflow obstruction or bronchospasm. Interpret with oxygenation, work of breathing, and response to therapy.

Example: O2 Glow Dim

Fantasy:

The patient’s oxygen glow is fading.

Simple Medical:

Oxygen may be low or the patient may not be breathing effectively.

Nursing:

This suggests possible decreased oxygenation or increased work of breathing.

NCLEX:

This is a cue related to oxygenation. Prioritize airway, breathing, respiratory effort, oxygen saturation, and response to intervention.

Professional:

Possible oxygenation concern. Correlate with respiratory effort, saturation, perfusion, and trend.

Professional:

Wrong Action by Difficulty

Guided:

This helped only a little. The clues point more toward breathing. Try revealing a clue or using an Air action.

Standard:

Limited effect. This action does not match the main pattern.

Clinical:

Poor clinical fit. Reassess the cues and identify the priority system.

NCLEX:

Intervention does not address the priority cues. Re-evaluate data before taking action.

Expert:

Unsafe or low-yield action. Patient condition may worsen if priority is missed.

Chapter Scaling

As the game progresses, difficulty should increase naturally even if the player stays on Standard.

Chapter 1: Foundation

Mostly single-system enemies.
2 to 4 clues.
Clear system identity.
Low penalty for wrong actions.
Care chain is short.
Goal: teach the loop.

Examples:

Air Sprite = asthma/bronchospasm
Energy Lock = hypoglycemia
Fire Imp = infection/wound infection
Bonegnaw Sprain = injury/fall risk

Chapter 2: Pattern Recognition

More clues are hidden.
Some enemies have related systems.
Wrong actions matter more.
Reassessment becomes more important.

Examples:

Pneumonia = Air + Fire
Dehydration = River + Filter
Hyperglycemia = Energy + River
Pressure Injury = Protection + Fire

Chapter 3: Priority Conflicts

Two problems may be present.
Player must decide priority.
Some actions help one meter but worsen another.
AP pressure increases.

Examples:

Heart failure fluid overload = River + Air
Sepsis intro = Fire + River
Delirium fall risk = Mind + Protection
DKA intro = Energy + Storm + River

Chapter 4: Evolving Conditions

Clues change over time.
Trends matter.
Reassessment can reveal worsening or improvement.
Delayed complications appear.

Examples:

Oxygenation worsens if Air problem ignored.
Blood pressure drops if infection spreads.
Confusion worsens if glucose remains low.

Chapter 5: Advanced Clinical Judgment

Mixed systems.
Distractor clues.
Prioritization required.
Some treatments are unsafe in certain contexts.
Boss battles require full care planning.

Examples:

COPD with infection
Sepsis with hypotension
Hypoglycemia with altered mental status
Heart failure with fluid overload
Electrolyte rhythm disturbance

---

Advanced Difficulty Mechanics for Later Chapters

Prepare for these mechanics later. Do not implement all at once unless safe.

1. Evolving Clues

Clues appear or worsen after turns.

Example:

Turn 1: Breathing Fast
Turn 2: O2 Glow Dim
Turn 3: Exhaustion appears if not stabilized

2. Trend Clues

Reassessment reveals whether condition improves or worsens.

Example:

After Airway Rune:
Wheezing improved.
O2 Glow brighter.
Stability recovering.

or

After wrong action:
Breathing remains fast.
O2 Glow worsens.

3. Distractor Clues

Advanced enemies may include clues that are real but not priority.

Example:

Mild anxiety appears during asthma, but airway/breathing remains priority.

4. Unsafe Action Conditions

Some actions become unsafe when certain clues are present.

Example:

River Bolus is helpful for low BP/hypovolemia.
River Bolus is unsafe when Crackles, Leg Swelling, or BNP clue suggests fluid overload.

5. Multi-System Enemies

Enemy can have primary and secondary systems.

Example:

enemy.primarySystem = "Fire";
enemy.secondarySystems = ["River"];

For sepsis:

Fire = infection source
River = perfusion/BP risk

6. Priority Shift

Enemy priority can change mid-battle.

Example:

At first, infection source is priority.
If Stability drops below 30, circulation becomes immediate priority.

7. Limited Assessment Actions

On higher difficulty, Scout may reveal only one clue at a time.

Example:

Guided Scout reveals key clue.
Standard Scout reveals one hidden clue.
Clinical Scout reveals one clue but not interpretation.
NCLEX Scout reveals raw data only.
Expert Scout may require choosing assessment type.

Ethical and Safety Language

Because this is health education, include a simple disclaimer in Settings, Codex, or About.

Use:

Clinica is an educational game for learning body systems and clinical reasoning. It is not medical advice and does not replace professional training, clinical judgment, or licensed care.

For medication-like or treatment-like actions, use safe wording:

as ordered
per protocol
anticipate
support
monitor
reassess

Avoid unsafe wording that implies independent prescribing.

Overload casual players with NCLEX language.

Use only medical jargon with no fantasy hook.
Make difficulty only increase enemy HP.
Hide all clues from casual players.
Make the first battle impossible for NCLEX users.
Lock story behind high difficulty.
Remove explanations from harder modes.
Punish learning too aggressively.
Make every enemy multi-system in early chapters.
Make wrong actions always cause instant failure.
Oversexualize nurse or healer characters.
Copy existing anime/game IP.
Make early learning depend on rare summons.
Require duplicates to complete beginner battles.
Add payment now.
Add paid power now.
Add paid stamina refills now.
Make the UI too dark or too pixelated to read.
Hide the game purpose inside the Codex only.

Do:

Use clue visibility as the main difficulty lever.
Use hints less as difficulty increases.
Use enemy behavior more carefully in later chapters.
Keep early game teachable.
Let players change difficulty.
Reward higher difficulty modestly.
Let NCLEX players practice with all clues hidden if they choose.
Keep fantasy and medicine connected.
Keep vitals and clues readable.
Keep the first-time experience clear.

Final Design Intent

Clinica should not choose between anime fantasy and medical education.

It should combine them.

The player should feel:

I am playing a fantasy RPG.
I am restoring a body kingdom.
I am collecting healer heroes.
I am learning real medical terms.
I understand hard concepts because the game makes them visual and memorable.

For NCLEX players, the game should feel like:

I am practicing clinical judgment without staring at another question bank.

For nonmedical players, the game should feel like:

I am learning how the body works without feeling lost.

For anime/RPG players, the game should feel like:

I want to meet more healers, unlock new regions, and cleanse the next disease spirit.

For expert players, the game should feel like:

I need to assess, prioritize, intervene, and reassess as the patient condition changes.
